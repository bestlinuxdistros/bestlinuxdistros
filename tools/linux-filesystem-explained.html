Storage Primer

Linux Filesystems Explained

Pick the filesystem that matches your workload before you run the installer. This cheat sheet references popular distros inside our catalog.

Filesystem Best For Pros Watch Outs Popular Distros
EXT4 General desktops, servers Stable, fast, widely supported No native snapshots or compression Ubuntu, Debian, Mint
Btrfs Snapshot-loving desktops Copy-on-write, compression, send/receive Needs maintenance (balance/scrub) EndeavourOS, Garuda, openSUSE
XFS Servers with huge files Great throughput, online defrag No shrink support, metadata heavy RHEL, AlmaLinux, Rocky Linux
ZFS Workstations, NAS, VM hosts Checksums, snapshots, ARC caching Licensing friction, RAM hungry Pop!_OS (experimental), Clear Linux (community), TrueNAS

Choosing strategy

  1. Check installer support: some distros hide Btrfs/ZFS behind advanced toggles.
  2. Align with backup tooling: Timeshift loves EXT4/Btrfs, ZFS works best with zfs send.
  3. Plan future migrations. If you dual-boot Windows, EXT4 on its own partition remains easiest.

Still unsure? Revisit the distro selection guide and the performance tuning guide to see how filesystem choices affect benchmarks.
